Headspring is actually part of a project where we're helping to pioneer Agile in government to some degree. One of the biggest challenges with Agile in government is the procurement vehicles that government contractors have to follow or use in order to work with a government agency or an entity in government. Government agencies are used to the traditional PMI approach to project management and the waterfall methodology. In Texas there is a state mandated delivery process that all projects over a certain size have to follow which is effectively a waterfall process wherein everything is defined upfront. The biggest challenge right now is the procurement vehicles. Example: An agency wants to write a P.O. for $5,000,000 or $10,000,000 for a project in year one expecting the funds will provide to them everything they are going to need over the next three years.

Why is this problematic?

In government particularly, what I've seen is that most of the times stakeholders dump everything that they originally thought they were going to get for the price they thought they were going to get it at (i.e. most allotted budget and time is spent developing features just to reach an admission that the original features, as specified, were flawed), and so the project goes over budget and the stakeholders settle for either less than what they paid for or something different as they have learned a lot along the way. That is the biggest challenge right now. If you look at Texas state government contracts through The Department of Information Resources, there is nothing really in those contracts, besides staff augmentation contracts, that allows for the variable scope approach of the Agile methodology (or contracting for capacity). In government, it's institutionalized to think of all projects similar to, for example, maybe the way a road might be built for a county wherein there is just:
  • a design
  • full documentation that can be produced upfront
  • etc.
In reality, in software there are a lot of reasons why it's very difficult to come up with years' worth of requirements before beginning development, and despite anyone's best efforts, we're talking about creating the plans for something with the sophistication level of perhaps an aircraft carrier. It is not a trivial thing. Getting somebody within a government agency to actually approve the would-be upfront requirements for a multiyear project and bet that they are in fact 100% accurate (no changes required) over such a long period of time is an unfair request of them. The upfront plans simply aren't 100% accurate in such a scenario. The kind of people that know what the software needs to do are not the same kinds of people that can evaluate 5,000 or 10,000 pages of requirements documentation for accuracy, which is what one is looking at scope-wise for such a long term project to really fully define things.
Does this well scare you?
It scares many in government.
Without that precedent of accepting something in that regard, then what one is left with is a lot of smaller projects as we discussed before, which could greatly mitigate risk, but which government agencies are hesitant to sign up for because the procurement process itself is so onerous. There are so many steps involved that nobody wants to "go to the well" with regularity. Because it is so difficult to draw up a bucket of water, government stakeholders would rather just bring everything up at once, but in doing so they've greatly increased the risk of failure.

Bleak prognosis. Is there any good news?

Yes. As a software vendor for a government project, you may have to sign a large contract to engage with an agency, but ultimately the agency is the arbiter of success or failure for custom application development and so the stakeholders are not going to be totally inflexible to what they'll accept and to setting priorities. They can't be. Executive involvement though is absolutely key to success. One of the things I've heard before is that large waterfall projects eventually become Agile as the initial requirements become insufficient to meet needs, and so, essentially you will get an Agile project eventually with some degree of flexibility in reaching for requirements. The key to having the eventual shift towards Agile processes go well is having a good executive sponsorship. Having a good recognition of the transition to Agile that will appear and the pain that will cause it to manifest is also very helpful. When the waterfall process starts failing it is best that you:
  1. are not surprised
  2. do not consider it a bad thing
  3. don't panic
You actually can apply Agile within a more traditional contract.
