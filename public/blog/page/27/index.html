    Javascript First-letter Mystery

    I was working on producing something that had to look like this:

    I though I’d be clever and avoid having to wrap the ! in a span by using the pseudo-element first-letter to grab that initial exclamation point. first-element selects the first letter of the block it used

    on. Sounds perfect, right? Well, when I tried it I got:

    This was not at all what I wanted! It looked not only odd but clearly wrong - first doesn’t mean first and second! I tried playing with spacing and a few other things, and finally produced the following jsfiddle to simply illustrate the problem:

     

    With the jsfiddle at hand, I tweeted my confusion:

    In moments, I received a dual barrage of twitter responses: half reponding in amazement/surprise, and half with the actual specification of first-letter and the reason for what I was seeing.

    It turns out that, though odd, the behavior I was seesing was reasonably to spec. “Punctuation (i.e, characters defined in Unicode in the “open” (Ps), “close” (Pe), “initial” (Pi). “final” (Pf) and “other” (Po) punctuation classes), that precedes or follows the first letter should be included.”[1] It really is less obvious then I’d thought: see https://developer.mozilla.org/En/CSS/::first-letter for a whole discussion of first-letter subtleties: But browsers must be a little confused too: they all act a little bit different in this situation:

    Chrome

    (Note the blue ! and O)

    Firefox

    (Note that neither the ! or O are blue)

    IE something

    (Note that the ! is blue, but the O is not - what I actually wanted!)

    Opera

    (Interestingly, Opera matches IE here.)

    In summary, I’ve learned that css is a little weirder than I had thought, and when they say letter, they really mean letter. Mostly. Sorta.

    [1] http://www.w3.org/TR/css3-selectors/#first-letter

    Headspring Named One of Austin’s Best Places to Work

    Leadership team expected to attend ceremony for winners of Austin Business Journal’s 2012 Best Places to Work

    Austin, TX - May 2012, The Austin Business Journal has named Headspring as one of the Best Places to Work in Austin, within the small business category.

    Companies selected for the list were evaluated according to climate, team atmosphere, benefits, and employee engagement. The list is compiled based on data from voluntary employee survey results, and sorted according to company size. Rankings for each company size category will be announced at a ceremony hosted by the Austin Business Journal at the AT&T Executive Education & Conference Center on June 6, 2012.  Headspring CEO/Founder Dustin Wells and President Jeffrey Palermo are expected to attend.

    “It is such an honor to be named one of the 2012 Best Places to Work in Austin,” says Dustin Wells. “Austin is a rapidly growing city with an expansive job market; job seekers have a lot of options.  We are really touched by the fact that our talented team of employees choose to work here with us and evangelize on behalf of our unique culture.”

    For more information on the Austin Business Journal’s Best Places to Work, visit: http://www.bizjournals.com/austin/event/58021

    CQRS With Kevin Hurwitz and Jeffrey Palermo

    In this cast, Kevin Hurwitz (Chief Architect) discusses the Command Query Responsibility Segregation pattern with Jeffrey Palermo (President/COO).  We have used CQRS successfully in several development projects for clients, and we have learned when it is appropriate and when it is not.  This cast distills the pattern down to its simplest essence and attempts to talk concretely about what is different from applications that employ CQRS.  Here are some other resources.

    Database Migrations With Kevin Hurwitz and Jeffrey Palermo

    In this cast, Kevin Hurwitz (Chief Architect) discusses database migrations and database change management with Jeffrey Palermo (President/COO) while covering the problem, options, and our recommended approach.  We use an open source tool called Tarantino in our deployment-based process.  This tool can be executed via a NAnt or Psake build script as well as stand-alone from the command line.  Here are some additional resources.

    Headspringers at Austin Code Camp

    Austin Code Camp has become a running tradition in the Austin area.  I still have a t-shirt from 2007 I occasionally mow the yard in!  While Austin Code Camp doesn’t give out t-shirts anymore, it’s still focused on providing FREE sessions for the development community, by members of the community.  This year’s event is going to be June 9th.  You can register over at http://austincodecamp2012.com/.

    A number of Headspringers are going to be presenting sessions this year.  The subjects cover a lot of varied ground - from introductions to deep dives, and focuses ranging from managing technology to managing your career.

    Here’s a list of what Headspringers are going to be presenting or facilitating:

    Steve Donie – Client Side JavaScript Frameworks
    Sharon Cichelli - Crafting your Career
    Nolan Egly - Cryptography 101
    Alonso Robles - Getting Started with Raven DB
    Patrick Lioi - Building a Language
    Tim Thomas - Avoiding the Threat of Awful CSS, Web Usability on a Budget
    Anne Epstein - Don’t Do This in NHibernate, Lightning Talks!
    Jeffrey Palermo - Star Schema and Reports, Onion Architecture

    We hope to see you there! (Did I mention it’s FREE?)
